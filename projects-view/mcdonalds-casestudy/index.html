Overview

What this project is about

Fast food chains generate large volumes of nutritional data across hundreds of menu items, but without structure that data rarely tells a clear health story. This project takes a real multi-chain fast food dataset — covering eight restaurants, 515 total items, and 17 nutritional attributes — and focuses it on McDonald's to surface meaningful patterns.


The goal was to diagnose McDonald's menu nutritionally: which categories carry the most risk, where does sodium become dangerous, how does the menu compare to competitors, and what does the "healthy" segment actually look like? Every query was designed to answer a specific question, and the dashboard was built to make those answers visible at a glance.

Phase 01

SQL Analysis in MySQL

Before any dashboard, the data had to be interrogated at the query level. Using MySQL Workbench and a live remote database connection, I worked through ten structured analytical queries — each designed to pull a specific nutritional insight from the dataset. The queries built a layered picture, from simple aggregations to category-level and cross-chain comparisons.


Precision mattered at every step. Working with columns like cal_fat and total_fat — which look similar but represent entirely different things — required carefully deciding what was actually being measured before writing a single line. That discipline shaped the entire analytical approach.

Diagnostic Findings

Five critical findings emerged from the analysis — each backed by both SQL queries and Power BI visuals. Together they build a complete nutritional picture of the McDonald's menu.

Calorie Distribution Across the Menu
42% cluster in the 300–600 kcal range
The majority of McDonald's menu sits in the mid-calorie 400–700 kcal band, which is acceptable by most standards. Competitive
McDonald's vs Competitors — Calorie Ranking
640 kcal avg · #1 across all 8 chains
McDonald's leads all eight chains in average calories per item at 640 kcal — sitting 67% above Chick Fil-A's 384 kcal average. Simultaneously, it holds the highest average protein across all chains at 40.3g per item. This positions it as nutritionally extreme in both directions: the most calorie-dense, and the highest protein output. Whether that tradeoff is acceptable depends entirely on which items you choose.

Only 11 of 57 items pass both criteria
Using a dual threshold of under 500 kcal and under 1,000mg sodium, only 19% of the McDonald's menu can be considered "healthier." Salads make up the most reliable group. The absolute safest item is the Premium Asian Salad without Chicken — just 140 kcal and 20mg sodium.

Phase 03

Power BI Dashboard

With the SQL analysis complete, the insights were brought to life in Power BI via a live ODBC connection to MySQL — meaning the dashboard pulls directly from the database rather than a static export. DAX measures were written to calculate calorie band classification, sodium risk flags, protein ratios, and healthy item thresholds. The result is a fully interactive report usable in presentations or stakeholder reviews.

Insights

What the data revealed

The analysis surfaced patterns that matter for anyone thinking about public health, menu strategy, or responsible consumption. Six core takeaways emerged across the SQL and dashboard layers.

McDonald's is the Calorie Leader
At 640 kcal per item average, McDonald's sits at the top across all eight chains — 67% higher than Chick Fil-A's 384 kcal. A single average item consumes nearly a third of the standard 2,000 kcal daily target.
Sodium is the Primary Risk Factor
Average sodium per item is 1,438mg — already 62% of the recommended daily 2,300mg limit. 74% of items exceed 1,000mg, and the worst item delivers 6,080mg: 2.6× the entire daily limit in one order.
Chicken Drives Both Volume and Risk
Chicken is 54% of the menu yet averages 2.3× the sodium and 1.7× the calories of salads. Tenders dominate every high-calorie and high-sodium ranking, making them the segment with the worst nutritional profile by far.
Salads are the Only Consistently Safe Category
Salads average 332 kcal and 824mg sodium — the only category where eating within daily guidelines is consistently possible. Only 11 of 57 items pass the dual threshold of under 500 kcal and under 1,000mg sodium.
Burgers Have the Best Protein Efficiency
Burgers offer the best protein-to-sodium ratio — 34.72% protein share vs 61.55% sodium share. Tenders are the inverse: lowest protein ratio (26.78%) and the highest sodium concentration (69.90%) of any category.
Calorie Distribution Skews Mid-to-High
42% of items cluster in the 400–700 kcal band, 28% exceed 700 kcal, and 14% breach 900 kcal. Only 16% sit under 300 kcal — showing the genuinely low-calorie end is a small, specific part of a mostly heavy menu.

Reflection

What this project taught me

🔍
Column names don't tell the whole story
Working with a raw nutritional dataset means confronting columns like cal_fat and total_fat that look similar but represent entirely different things. Deciding which to use — and why — before writing a single query is now an instinct I carry forward to every project.
🔗
Live connections change how you think about reports
Setting up an ODBC connection from MySQL to Power BI meant the dashboard wasn't a static screenshot — it was a live view into the database. That shift from export-based to connection-based thinking changes how you design both the SQL layer and the report on top of it.
📊
The insight only lands when the framing is right
Knowing the 20pc Crispy Tenders contain 6,080mg of sodium is a raw fact. Saying it's 2.6× the entire recommended daily limit is an insight. The difference is context — and building that context into DAX measures and visual labels is where analysis actually becomes communication.
